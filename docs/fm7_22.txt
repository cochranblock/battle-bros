Chapter 1 - Army Physical Readiness Training Overview
Section 1-1 - Purpose
FM 7-22 prescribes doctrine for the Army Physical Readiness Training (PRT) program. PRT provides Soldiers with the physical readiness required to accomplish any combat or military task. The PRT program is designed to improve and maintain a baseline of physical readiness that enables Soldiers to carry out the Army mission.

Section 1-2 - Physical Readiness
Physical readiness is the ability to meet the physical demands of any combat or duty position, accomplish the mission, and continue to fight and win. It encompasses the development and maintenance of strength, endurance, and mobility.

Section 1-3 - Principles of Training
The principles of exercise — progression, regularity, overload, balance, specificity, and variety — are the foundation of physical readiness training. Commanders must use these principles when designing PRT programs.

Chapter 2 - System for Physical Readiness Training
Section 2-1 - Types of PRT Programs
There are three types of PRT programs: initial conditioning (toughening phase), sustaining phase, and reconditioning phase. Each targets different populations based on their current physical readiness level.

Section 2-2 - Toughening Phase
The toughening phase targets Soldiers with low fitness levels. Initial Military Training (IMT) uses the toughening phase to prepare new Soldiers for the rigors of military service.

Section 2-3 - Sustaining Phase
The sustaining phase targets unit populations with average to high fitness levels. The goal is to maintain and improve physical readiness for combat operations.

Section 2-4 - Reconditioning Phase
The reconditioning phase targets Soldiers who have been injured, ill, or otherwise unable to maintain their physical readiness. The goal is to safely return Soldiers to full duty.

Chapter 5 - Army Combat Fitness Test
Section 5-1 - Purpose of the ACFT
The Army Combat Fitness Test (ACFT) is the test of record for the Army. The ACFT assesses the physical readiness of Soldiers to perform common Soldier tasks. It replaces the Army Physical Fitness Test (APFT).

Section 5-2 - ACFT Events
The ACFT consists of six events: 3 Repetition Maximum Deadlift (MDL), Standing Power Throw (SPT), Hand Release Push-Up (HRP), Sprint-Drag-Carry (SDC), Plank (PLK), and Two-Mile Run (2MR). Each event measures a component of fitness required for combat operations.

Section 5-3 - ACFT Standards
Soldiers must achieve a minimum score in each event to pass the ACFT. Scoring is based on a 0-100 point scale per event. A minimum of 60 points per event is required to pass.

Section 5-4 - ACFT Administration
The ACFT will be administered by trained and certified graders. Unit commanders are responsible for ensuring Soldiers are tested at least twice per year. Soldiers who fail the ACFT will be enrolled in remedial PRT programs.

Chapter 7 - Nutrition
Section 7-1 - Role of Nutrition in Physical Readiness
Proper nutrition is essential for physical readiness. Soldiers must consume adequate calories, macronutrients, and micronutrients to support training, recovery, and mission performance.

Section 7-2 - Hydration
Adequate hydration is critical for performance and health. Soldiers should consume water before, during, and after physical activity. Dehydration degrades physical and cognitive performance.

Chapter 10 - Injury Prevention
Section 10-1 - Risk Factors for Injury
Common risk factors for training injuries include overtraining, improper technique, inadequate recovery, and pre-existing conditions. Leaders must monitor training volume and intensity to prevent injuries.

Section 10-2 - Commander Responsibilities
Commanders are responsible for implementing injury prevention programs. This includes proper warm-up and cool-down procedures, progressive training loads, and access to medical support.
